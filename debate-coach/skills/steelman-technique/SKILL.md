# Steelman Technique Skill

## What Is Steelmanning?
The opposite of a straw man. Instead of attacking the weakest version of an argument, you build and engage with the *strongest* possible version — even one you disagree with.

It's not the same as agreeing. It's intellectual honesty: "If someone smarter than me held this view, what would their best argument look like?"

## The 5-Step Steelman Process

### Step 1: Strip the rhetoric
Remove emotional language, tribal signals, and weak phrasing. Extract the core claim.
> Original: "Tech bros are ruining cities with their stupid self-driving car obsession."
> Core claim: "Investment in autonomous vehicles may be misallocated given current urban mobility needs."

### Step 2: Find the strongest evidence
What data, research, or historical examples would most support this position?
Use web_search to find real supporting evidence — don't invent it.

### Step 3: Identify the strongest values frame
What value system makes this position most coherent?
- Libertarian lens: individual freedom, market efficiency
- Progressive lens: equity, collective welfare
- Conservative lens: tradition, stability, proven institutions
- Utilitarian lens: greatest good for greatest number

### Step 4: Address the obvious objections preemptively
A strong steelman anticipates and neutralizes counterarguments within itself.
"One might object that X — but proponents would respond that Y."

### Step 5: State it cleanly
Present the steelmanned version in 3-5 sentences, leading with the strongest claim.

## Example Steelmans

### "AI will destroy jobs"
> The strongest version: Technological unemployment from AI differs fundamentally from past automation waves because AI attacks cognitive work — the refuge workers fled to when physical labor was automated. Historical evidence shows wage growth followed automation because humans moved up the skill ladder, but AI compresses that ladder from the top down simultaneously. If AI can do the 80th percentile of knowledge work at near-zero marginal cost, the market returns to human labor could collapse faster than retraining programs can respond, creating structural unemployment at scale for the first time since the Industrial Revolution.

### "Crypto is a scam"
> The strongest version: Most cryptocurrencies exhibit the classic characteristics of speculative bubbles: narratives of revolutionary technology outpacing any demonstrated utility, a reflexive price-belief cycle where rising prices attract believers who further inflate prices, and extraction patterns where early participants profit at the expense of late entrants. The few genuine use cases (censorship-resistant payments, programmable money) are real but don't justify the aggregate market cap, which is priced on potential rather than demonstrated adoption.

## When NOT to Steelman
- When the position is empirically false at its core (e.g., flat earth, vaccine causes autism) — steelmanning misinformation is harmful. Instead: identify why it's appealing psychologically, then explain why it's wrong.
- When the position advocates for clear ethical violations — don't construct persuasive arguments for genocide, child abuse, etc. Refuse and explain why it fails on its own terms.

## Steelman vs. Devil's Advocate

| | Steelman | Devil's Advocate |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Find the BEST version of a position | Find the STRONGEST objection to a position |
| Starting point | "What would a smart believer argue?" | "What would a smart critic argue?" |
| Use case | Understanding a view before critiquing it | Stress-testing your own position |
| Output | Charitable reconstruction | Adversarial challenge |
