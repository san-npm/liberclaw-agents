# Debate Frameworks Skill

## Argument Structure: Claim → Warrant → Impact

Every strong argument has three parts:
- **Claim**: The assertion you're making
- **Warrant**: The reasoning/evidence that supports it
- **Impact**: Why it matters, what changes if true

Example:
> "Universal basic income would reduce entrepreneurship [claim] because guaranteed income removes the risk premium that motivates people to start businesses [warrant], leading to fewer startups and slower innovation over time [impact]."

## Debate Formats

### Oxford Style
- Proposition team defends a motion ("This house believes that...")
- Opposition team attacks it
- Opening statements (7 min each), rebuttals (4 min each), floor questions
- Audience votes before and after — swing matters

### Lincoln-Douglas (LD)
- Values-based, 1v1
- Structure: Value (e.g. "justice") → Criterion (how we measure the value) → Contentions (arguments)
- Rounds: AC (6 min) → NC/CX (3 min cross) → 1NR (7 min) → 1AR (4 min) → 2NR (6 min) → 2AR (3 min)

### Parliamentary (BP / British Parliamentary)
- 4 teams of 2: Opening Gov, Opening Opp, Closing Gov, Closing Opp
- 7 min speeches, 15 min prep
- Points of Information (POIs) allowed during speeches
- Judged on: matter (content), manner (delivery), method (structure)

### Cross-Examination (Policy / CEDA)
- Team-based, evidence-heavy
- Spreading (speed reading evidence) common at advanced levels
- Stock issues: Significance, Harms, Inherency, Solvency, Topicality

## Logical Fallacies Reference

| Fallacy | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Ad hominem | Attack the person, not the argument | "You can't trust his climate views, he's not a scientist" |
| Straw man | Misrepresent opponent's position to attack a weaker version | "You want to reduce military spending? So you want us to have no defense?" |
| False dichotomy | Present only two options when more exist | "You're either with us or against us" |
| Slippery slope | Assume one step inevitably leads to extreme outcomes | "If we allow gay marriage, next they'll want to marry animals" |
| Appeal to authority | Use authority as evidence without the argument | "Einstein believed in God, therefore God exists" |
| Correlation = causation | Assume relationship implies cause | "Ice cream sales and drowning both rise in summer, so ice cream causes drowning" |
| Hasty generalization | Draw broad conclusions from small samples | "I met two rude Parisians, therefore the French are rude" |
| Begging the question | Conclusion is assumed in the premise | "The Bible is true because it says so in the Bible" |
| Moving goalposts | Change requirements after they've been met | "Okay you proved X, but now show me Y too" |
| Gish gallop | Overwhelm with many weak arguments so opponent can't address all | Rapid-fire 10 bad points in a row |
| Appeal to nature | Natural = good, artificial = bad | "This supplement is all-natural, so it must be safe" |
| No true Scotsman | Exclude counterexamples by redefining the category | "No real socialist would do that" |

## Rebuttal Techniques

### The DARE Formula
- **D**eny: "This claim is false because..."
- **A**ttack: "And this actually supports our side because..."
- **R**edirect: "The real question is..."
- **E**xtend: "And even if true, it doesn't matter because..."

### Turning Arguments
When the opponent's argument can be flipped to support your side:
"They argue X causes Y — but Y actually supports our position because..."

### Concede and Minimize
"Even granting that X is true, it doesn't reach the threshold needed to justify their conclusion because..."
