# Skill: Marketing Strategy

ICP → JTBD → positioning → messaging → channel mix → OKRs. The frame the agent uses before it touches tactics. Refuses to do tactics work without knowing the strategy — or flags the gap explicitly.

---

## Starting order (skip at your peril)

1. **ICP** — who are we for, specifically
2. **JTBD** — what job they're hiring us to do
3. **Positioning** — what category we're in, relative to what
4. **Messaging** — how we say it
5. **Channels** — where they live, where we show up
6. **Offer + Pricing** — what they can say yes to, at what price
7. **OKRs** — what we'll measure for the next 90 days

If the user jumps straight to channels or tactics, pull them back one or two steps. The most common cause of wasted growth spend is tactics executed cleanly against a blurry ICP.

---

## 1 · ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)

Not a persona poster with a stock photo. A falsifiable filter.

### Quantitative (who, observably)
- Firmographic: company size (employees + revenue), industry, geography, maturity (seed / series A / public), tech stack
- Demographic (B2B): role / seniority / reporting line / budget authority
- Behavioural: current tools they use, recent triggers (new funding, leadership change, compliance deadline)

### Qualitative (why, from interviews)
- 5–10 user interviews with recent customers (not prospects — actual converters)
- Ask: "Tell me about the day you decided to solve this" (JTBD timeline)
- Ask: "What did you try before us, and why did those fail?"
- Ask: "What happened in the 24h before you bought?"
- Record verbatim language — not summaries. Your messaging lives in their quotes.

### ICP scorecard
Any deal in progress can be scored 0–5 on each dimension. Low-scoring deals get slower onboarding / more scrutiny / higher prices. Raise the bar as the business grows — today's ICP is usually yesterday's best deals, not tomorrow's.

### Red flags in an ICP doc
- Multiple ICPs at an early-stage company ("we serve SMBs AND enterprises") — usually means the team hasn't chosen
- ICP defined by features used instead of problem owned
- ICP written without talking to real customers

## 2 · Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

Customers don't buy products; they hire them to make progress on a job.

### The canonical JTBD statement
```
When {SITUATION},
I want to {MOTIVATION},
so I can {EXPECTED OUTCOME}.
```

Example (a Luxembourg SME funding tool):
```
When I realise I may qualify for a public grant I didn't know existed,
I want to see within 5 minutes which programmes I match and how to apply,
so I can pursue free-money options before committing to dilutive funding.
```

### The forces of progress
For every switching decision:
- **Push** of the current situation (what's broken)
- **Pull** of the new solution (what's promised)
- **Anxiety** about switching (what could go wrong)
- **Habit** of the current way (inertia)

Strong marketing amplifies push + pull and reduces anxiety. Messaging that ignores anxiety fails — especially in B2B with procurement.

## 3 · Positioning (April Dunford's 5 steps)

1. **Competitive alternatives** — what would customers use if you didn't exist? (Often: a spreadsheet, a junior hire, or doing nothing — not always a named competitor.)
2. **Unique attributes** — what do you have that they don't? (Features, data, integrations, pricing model, geography, compliance.)
3. **Value (so what?)** — what those attributes enable for the customer. "Fast" isn't a value. "Ship a compliance audit in 1 day instead of 6 weeks" is.
4. **Best-fit customers** — who gets the most value. Same as the ICP.
5. **Market category** — the frame you're in. Choose deliberately. An established category = easier to explain, harder to stand out. A new category = explaining costs, but defensible.

### Output: a one-page positioning doc
- One sentence: `{Product} is the {category} for {best-fit customer} who want {value} because we're the only one that {unique attribute}.`
- The 5 sections above
- A short explainer for how you're NOT another {obvious comparison}

Revisit yearly. Positioning is not set-and-forget.

## 4 · Messaging

### Hierarchy
1. **Promise** — one-sentence value to the best-fit customer
2. **Proofs** — 3–5 reasons-to-believe (data, customer quotes, logos, awards, certifications)
3. **Pillars** — 3 content themes that support the promise over time
4. **Anti-positioning** — who you're NOT for, stated explicitly

### Copy rules
- Write for the lowest-context buyer in your ICP, not the internal expert
- Cut jargon. "Synergistic value orchestration" fails every reader.
- Specific > abstract: "Close your books 4 days faster" > "Accelerate finance workflows"
- Active voice
- Reference the ICP's situation, not the product's features, in headlines

### Message testing
- Ads (LinkedIn / Meta) are the cheapest test harness — CTR and CPC diverge sharply between weak and strong messages within 48h
- Run 3 variants against the same audience, same creative, message-only change
- Winner becomes the hero message for the next quarter

## 5 · Channels

### Where to show up = where the ICP lives × where intent is high

- **Google organic** — high intent, high effort, 3–9 month payoff
- **Google Ads (Search)** — high intent, fast, cost scales with competition
- **LinkedIn organic** — B2B attention, compound with consistent posting (6–12 months to momentum)
- **LinkedIn Ads** — expensive, good for ABM + mid-market / enterprise
- **Meta Ads** — consumer + prosumer, less B2B unless the persona is on FB/Instagram
- **Reddit + niche communities** — organic, trust-heavy, hard to fake
- **YouTube** — long tail, high effort, strong for technical products
- **Podcasts** — as guest, low-cost authority; as host, slow compound
- **Email / newsletter** — owned channel, the last moat in a zero-click world
- **Partnerships / integrations** — leverage other people's distribution
- **Events + community** — high touch, high conversion, low scale
- **PR** — brand and trust, not leads; worth it for fundraising / category creation

### Channel selection rule
Pick 2 channels that match the ICP habitat and commit for 2 quarters. A fragmented spread across 6 channels always under-performs focused investment in 2.

### Channel-market fit check
For each candidate channel:
- Is the ICP verifiably there? (Evidence, not assumption.)
- Is intent high enough to convert?
- Is the unit economics viable at realistic CAC?
- Is it compounding (owned / earned) or spend-dependent (paid)?

Pay for paid, invest in owned, cultivate earned. A healthy growth stack has all three.

## 6 · Offer and pricing

### Pricing models
- **Flat** — simplest, transparent, hard to expand
- **Tiered (Good / Better / Best)** — default for SaaS; 80% choose Better
- **Per-seat** — scales with company growth; friction at budget time
- **Usage-based** — value-aligned; billing-anxiety risk
- **Value-based** — rare, highest margin; requires strong ROI proof
- **Hybrid** — platform fee + usage (Stripe, Snowflake pattern)

### Packaging rules
- Anchor on value delivered, not cost to deliver
- 3 tiers usually outperforms 4+ (decision fatigue)
- Free tier = acquisition channel, not revenue channel — designed to expose the pain the paid tier solves
- Annual discount 10–20% — improves retention and cash flow
- Custom / Enterprise tier with "contact sales" anchors perceived value of self-serve tiers

### Price testing
- New pricing → never retroactive on existing customers without a grandfather period
- A/B test pricing page at early stage (pre-product-market-fit) only if you have volume; otherwise use sequential launches + customer interviews

## 7 · Goals and OKRs

### Quarterly OKRs

One **Objective** (qualitative theme), 2–4 **Key Results** (specific, measurable, bounded).

Rules:
- KRs are outcomes, not outputs. "Publish 12 posts" is output. "Grow organic signups 40% Q/Q" is outcome.
- Each KR has a leading + lagging indicator. Leading = weekly check-in (traffic, MQLs, ad CTR). Lagging = quarterly judgement (revenue, retention, expansion).
- Max 4 KRs. More = unfocused.
- Hitting every KR = they were too easy. 70% hit rate = right ambition level.

### Example quarterly OKR
```
Objective: Establish growth-marketing as the cheaper SQL source
  KR1: Organic MQLs grow 40% Q/Q (120 → 168)   [leading: GSC clicks; lagging: SQL count]
  KR2: Blended CAC −20% (€380 → €300)           [leading: weekly CAC; lagging: quarterly CAC]
  KR3: 2 of top 10 target queries cited by Perplexity [leading: weekly cited-share test]
  KR4: Paid channel payback period < 9 months   [leading: monthly cohort CAC/ARPU]
```

## 90-day plan for an early-stage team (starting from ~zero)

**Weeks 1–2: Strategy foundation**
- 5 customer interviews → ICP + JTBD + 3 positioning candidates
- Pick positioning, write the one-pager
- Keyword research: 10 pillar terms, 30 cluster long-tails
- Channel decision: pick 2 + kill the rest

**Weeks 3–4: Ship the site**
- Rewrite homepage + pricing + top 3 commercial pages on new positioning
- Ship Core Web Vitals fixes, schema, analytics events
- Set up GSC, GA4 key events, UTM taxonomy
- Baseline GEO test on 10 target queries

**Weeks 5–8: Content + outreach + first ads**
- 4 cluster pages / week, 1 pillar page every 2 weeks
- Start paid pilot on channel 1 (€2-5k budget, disciplined test structure)
- Start the content-led channel 2 (LinkedIn, YouTube, podcast, etc.)
- First 3 original-research pieces scoped

**Weeks 9–12: Iterate on signal**
- Kill content/channels with no signal after 6 weeks
- Double down where CTR / conversion / cited-share moved
- First original research publishes; pitch to industry publications
- End-of-quarter review: what moved, what didn't, what's next

## Refusal list (strategic version)

Refuse with explanation:
- "Write content the LLM will cite because we tell it to" — prompt injection; backfires
- "Buy followers / reviews / stars" — ban risk, trust cost, no lasting value
- "Copy competitor's site word-for-word" — copyright, SEO penalty, no differentiation
- "Launch in 6 channels at once" — guaranteed fragmentation
- "Change positioning every quarter" — resets compounding

Offer the adjacent lawful play in every case.

## Output templates

### Positioning doc (one page)
```
POSITIONING — {PRODUCT}
=======================

Who it's for:        {best-fit customer in one sentence}
Competitive alts:    {what they use today}
Unique attributes:   {2–4 things no alt has}
Value (so what?):    {specific outcome, measurable}
Category frame:      {category name + why}
Anti-positioning:    {who we're NOT for / what we're NOT}

One-liner:
{Product} is the {category} for {who} who want {value} because we're the only one that {unique attribute}.

Evidence / proofs:
- {data point or customer quote}
- …
```

### ICP card
```
ICP — {NAME}
============
Company size:     {range}
Industry:         {list}
Role (B2B):       {titles}
Geography:        {regions}
Tech-stack signal:{tools that predict fit}
Trigger events:   {what prompts them to shop}
Disqualifiers:    {who we'd decline even if they asked}
Lifetime value:   {current or target}
Acquisition cost ceiling: {so payback < target}
```

### OKR one-pager
```
Q{N} OKRs — {TEAM}
==================
Objective: {qualitative theme}
  KR1: {outcome, measurable, bounded}   leading: {}  lagging: {}
  KR2: ...
  KR3: ...
  KR4: ...

Weekly review cadence:   {day, who attends}
Mid-quarter checkpoint:  {week 6 — adjust or kill}
End-of-quarter review:   {what we'll ship next}
```
